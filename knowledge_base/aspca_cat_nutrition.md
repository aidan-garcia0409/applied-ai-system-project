# Cat Nutrition Guide
Source: ASPCA (aspca.org/pet-care/cat-care/cat-nutrition-tips)
Species: cat

## Essential Nutrients

Cats require six fundamental nutrient classes. Water is the most important nutrient, and cats need fresh clean water available at all times. Proteins serve as building blocks for cells and tissues, with animal-based proteins offering complete amino acid profiles.

Taurine is a critical amino acid only found in foods of animal origin, such as meat, eggs and fish. Taurine deficiency causes eye disease and dilated cardiomyopathy (heart disease) in cats. Cat food must always contain adequate taurine.

Fats provide concentrated energy and support hormone production and vitamin absorption. Carbohydrates supply energy for tissues, though cats have no minimum carbohydrate requirement. When feeding a complete and balanced diet, it is unnecessary to give a vitamin or mineral supplement unless a specific deficiency is diagnosed by a veterinarian.

## Feeding Schedules

The ASPCA recommends feeding adult cats twice daily using portion control, with meals spaced eight to twelve hours apart. Alternative methods include timed feeding (allowing 30 minutes to eat) or free-choice feeding with dry food left available continuously. Some cats overeat with free-choice feeding, potentially causing obesity.

## Key Restrictions

Never provide cow's milk, as it causes digestive upset in cats lacking sufficient lactase enzyme. Treats should comprise no more than 5% of daily nutritional intake. Onion and garlic are toxic to cats and must be avoided.

## Life Stage Considerations

Kittens need two to three times the energy of adults and require kitten-specific formulas until one year old. Senior cats should transition to senior diets around age seven to address age-related health changes and maintain optimal body weight.
