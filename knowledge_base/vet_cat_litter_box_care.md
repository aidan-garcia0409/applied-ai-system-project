# Cat Litter Box Care
Source: Curated from ASPCA and Cornell Feline Health Center published guidelines
Species: cat

## Why Litter Box Maintenance Is a High-Priority Task

Litter box hygiene is directly linked to a cat's physical and psychological health. Cats are fastidious animals with a strong aversion to soiled environments. A dirty litter box is one of the most common reasons cats begin eliminating outside the box — a behavior that is stressful for both cat and owner and difficult to reverse once established.

## Scooping Frequency

The litter box should be scooped at least once daily. Cats that share a box may require twice-daily scooping. Cats have an extremely sensitive sense of smell — roughly 14 times stronger than humans. A box that smells acceptable to a human may already be intolerable to a cat.

## Full Litter Changes

Even with daily scooping, the entire litter should be replaced completely once or twice per week for clumping litter. Non-clumping litter requires full replacement every 2-3 days. The box itself should be scrubbed with mild soap and hot water monthly. Avoid strong-smelling cleaners or disinfectants, which can deter cats from using the box.

## Number and Placement of Boxes

The standard recommendation is one litter box per cat, plus one extra. For example, two cats should have three boxes. Boxes should be placed in quiet, low-traffic locations where the cat can approach and exit from multiple directions. Avoid placing food and water near litter boxes — cats instinctively avoid eliminating near their food sources.

## Health Monitoring Through Litter Box Habits

Daily scooping provides an opportunity to monitor your cat's health. Changes in urine frequency, urine color, stool consistency, or signs of straining can indicate urinary tract infections, kidney disease, or gastrointestinal issues. Early detection during routine litter maintenance often leads to faster veterinary intervention and better outcomes.

## Litter Type Preferences

Most cats prefer unscented, fine-grained clumping litter that mimics natural soil. Heavily scented litters may be marketed to owners but are often aversive to cats. If switching litter types, transition gradually by mixing new and old litter over 1-2 weeks.
