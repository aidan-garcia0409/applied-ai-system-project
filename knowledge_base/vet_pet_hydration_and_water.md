# Pet Hydration and Water Refresh
Source: Curated from ASPCA and AVMA published guidelines
Species: dog, cat

## Why Fresh Water Is Critical

Water is the single most important nutrient for both dogs and cats. It accounts for 60-70% of an adult pet's body weight and is involved in every biological process: digestion, temperature regulation, joint lubrication, and waste elimination. A 10% reduction in body water causes serious illness; a 15% reduction can be fatal.

## How Often to Refresh Water

Water bowls should be refreshed with clean, fresh water at least once daily — twice daily is better, especially in warm weather. Standing water accumulates bacteria, saliva, and food debris quickly, particularly in warmer temperatures. Cats in particular are sensitive to water freshness and may reduce their intake if the water tastes or smells stale.

## Cats and Hydration Challenges

Cats evolved as desert animals and have a low natural thirst drive. They are biologically adapted to obtain most moisture from prey. This means domestic cats eating dry kibble are often in a chronic state of mild dehydration, which over time increases the risk of urinary tract infections, kidney stones, and chronic kidney disease — the leading cause of death in senior cats.

Strategies to increase cat hydration:
- Offer wet food as part of the diet (wet food is approximately 70-80% water)
- Use a cat water fountain, which provides moving water that many cats find more appealing
- Place multiple water stations in different rooms
- Keep bowls away from food and litter boxes

## Dogs and Hydration

Adult dogs require approximately one ounce of water per pound of body weight per day. A 40-pound dog needs roughly 40 ounces (about 5 cups) daily. Dogs exercising in warm weather require significantly more. Signs of dehydration include lethargy, dry gums, sunken eyes, and loss of skin elasticity (skin that does not spring back when gently pinched).

## Bowl Hygiene

Food and water dishes should be washed daily with soap and hot water. Stainless steel and ceramic bowls are easiest to keep clean and do not harbor bacteria in the way plastic bowls can. Plastic bowls can develop micro-scratches that trap bacteria and may cause chin acne in cats.
